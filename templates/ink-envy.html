Overview

Project Brief

Ink Envy was the second app I created as a part of the Career Foundry UX Immersion course. The initial idea was to help tattoo enthusiasts find inspiration for their next tattoo, but after conducting extensive research and interviewing a sample of my user demographic, the app expanded to include many features around traveling for both customers and artists.

Using Ink Envy, tattoo enthusiasts can stay up to date with their favorite artists’ work, book appointments, save photos for later inspiration, and opt in for notifications about traveling tattoo artists.

My role

Ink Envy was an entirely independent project. I was responsible for everything from researching the competitive market, to designing the finished user interface. I conducted research, strategized effective solutions, designed the the interface, and conducted user testing with my target audience at multiple stages.

The Challenge

Tattoos are incredibly popular - 36% of 18-25 year olds and 40% of 36-40 year old have tattoos. It is a 1.65 billion dollar business in America. The challenge was to create an app that helps users explore inspiration for tattoos, connect with artists, and reduce regret.

All statistics can be found here: https://www.statisticbrain.com/tattoo-statistics/

The Approach

Ink Envy was designed with a user-centric mobile first approach, based on heuristic research, validated assumptions and repeated user testing.

Competitive Research

Introduction

To begin the project, I began conducting extensive research into the existing market of Tattoo related apps. During this research I discovered two important direct competitors - InkHunter and Tattoodo. I then began to analyze their apps and marketing strategies in order to strategize ways my app could secure a place in the market.

InkHunter

A powerful augmented reality app that allows users to use their phone’s camera to test tattoo ideas in real time.

While the AR portion of InkHunter works very well and is simple to learn, the app is missing some major features that can cause friction in the user experience.

For example, the app advertises that users can “follow” artists, but the follow button just redirects users to the artists Instagram page. Even worse, the app allows users to continue as guests, but does not allow them to create accounts in app. Meaning, if you skip the create account section, you cannot go back and create an account unless you delete and re-download the app.

Tattoodo

A large desktop and mobile platform that helps tattoo artists display their work and connect with customers. Over 300,000 artists have signed up to display their work on Tattoodo and they have raised over $15 million in funding since 2013.

Tattoodo’s strengths lie in it’s booking platform. It is the only Tattoo focused app that allows users to connect with real tattoo artists in the app and book appointments directly with them.

However, the app has some significant usability problems. For one, the “profile” page’s purpose seems unclear and overlaps with the “Saved photos” page. The app also does not allow users to search by location and style at the same time, which can cause friction with the user experience.

Finally, the overall aesthetic of the app feels antiquated and uninspiring, which means the app could be surmounted by an app with a more friendly user experience and a more aesthetically pleasing User Interface.

User Research

Summary

Once I had completed my market study, I began researching my target audience by interviewing people in my netwok with tattoos or an interest in getting tattoos. This gave me further insights into their knowledge of existing apps, their needs, their goals, and their frustrations. I then began to analyze the data by creating an affinity map, three user personas, and two user journeys. Two user personas represented Customers or Users and One user Persona represented an Artist as I thought it was important to understand both perspectives.

Usability and A/B Testing

Introduction

Once I had finished creating a high fidelity prototype, I began designing a usability test to see how easily real people within my target audience could complete specific tasks within the app. All of the tests took place in person and lasted between 10-20 minutes.

I also ran an A/B preference test with 20 volunteers to see if users preferred a dark or light theme with 20 volunteers.

Script

For a detailed look at the Test Script, including a list of all tasks tested, feel free to explore the complete script here.

Methodology

All 6 tests were taken in person, on my personal computer, with an offline version of my app. All tests were recorded using Cloud.io screen recording software.

Results

After conducting 6 usability tests and 1 A/B preference test, I have found 2 high priority usability issues with my design, 2 medium priority issues, and 1 low priority issue. Moreover, I have found that users are equally in favor of a dark or light theme. While the light theme allows the colors and pictures to “pop” more, the dark theme is easier on the eyes and makes the app stand out more. Ideally, I would like to build both themes and allow users to switch back and forth, but for now, I will proceed with the dark theme.

Redefining the Design

Once I had finished conducting my usability tests, organizing the data, and ranking the errors by their level of severity, I began to refine the final designs for Ink Envy. This involved making significant changes to the Artist’s profile pages, changing icons, and adjusting the the contrast on some buttons to increase the accessibility of the app.

Change #1

  • I changed the booking icon to a button to emphasize it as a CTA.
  • I changed the messaging icon to make it more intuitive.
  • I split the name and the location of the artist into two lines to make it easier to differentiate between the two pieces of information.
  • I changed all of my navigation icons to more pixel perfect versions.
  • I enhanced the size of the text of the tags to make them more accessible.

Change #2

  • I changed the title of the screen to “Appointments”.
  • I added a message button to the booking card to make it easier to contact artists from your bookings page.
  • I increased the size of the copy on the bookings card to make it more accessible.
  • I changed the suggested artists menu to a Past Appointments menu. Despite not having evidince for this, I theorized that users with existing appointments would not want to be pressured into booking more appointments.
  • I made the past appointments a revolving carousel because it is more aesthetically pleasing.

Change #3

  • I added 3 new buttons, including a new Book Appointment CTA.
  • I added tags that help identify an artist’s specialties and allow users to explore similar artists more easily.
  • I added an outline for the star and dollar ratings to give users more context. This way, the ratings would have tangible meaning for the users.
